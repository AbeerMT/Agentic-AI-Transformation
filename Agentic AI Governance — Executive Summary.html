Your accountability
5 things you cannot delegate
Everything below these five sits with your technical and legal teams. These five sit with you.
1
Approve which decisions are non-delegable to AI
Explicitly classify which decisions must always have a human owner — regardless of what AI can technically do.
2
Name an owner for every agentic system
Every AI agent needs a named human accountable for its operation, outcomes, and authority to shut it down. No anonymous ownership.
3
Approve the oversight mode for each system
Decide whether each AI system requires human approval on every action, human monitoring with the ability to intervene, or human policy-setting with post-hoc audit.
4
Receive the value realisation report quarterly
If AI is not changing decision quality, speed, or cost — it is not transformation. You should be seeing that evidence every quarter.
5
Hold the override authority when escalation reaches you
Governance escalation must have a defined endpoint. That endpoint is you. Override authority must have clear trigger conditions.

6 domains your teams govern

The risk picture
What could go wrong — at your level
These are not technical failures. These are governance failures. They happen when accountability is unclear or absent.
⚖️
No named owner
An agent causes harm and no one is accountable. The regulator asks who owns it. There is no answer.
🔄
Uncontrolled execution
An agent sends customer communications, moves funds, or deploys changes without anyone knowing the damage limit.
📉
No value evidence
Millions spent on AI with no measurable change in decision quality, speed, or cost. Governance becomes a cost centre.
🚨
Escalation with no endpoint
A governance crisis escalates through the chain and reaches you with no predefined override authority or intervention options.
📋
Compliance theatre
Governance exists on paper but not in operation. The audit passes. The next incident reveals nothing was actually enforced.
👥
Culture failure
Technology deployed, governance designed, people unchanged. The programme fails because operators never became governors.
Board-visible metrics
How you know it is working
These are the four questions a quarterly governance report must answer. If your team cannot answer all four, governance is not operational.
↓
Decision cost
Cost per decision before AI vs after. Is the economics changing?
↑
Decision quality
Accuracy, error rate, reversal rate. Is quality measurably better?
⚡
Time-to-decision
Speed improvement with attribution to AI, not other changes.
🛡
Risk incidents
Number and severity of governance incidents. Trend direction matters.
Kill criteria: If an agent cannot demonstrate improvement against its baseline after a defined period, it must be reviewed, constrained, or decommissioned. This decision sits with the named owner and escalates to you.
Where to start
Minimum viable governance posture
Before any agent touches production, these five pillars must be in place. In this order.

Capability Assessment

2.1

Named Accountability

2.4
Domain 2 · Control
Human Oversight Mode Assigned

4.1
